You can contact the clinic on weekdays during working hours.

What happens when the clinic contacts you

The clinic reception staff will try and call you at least twice. The number might show as "unknown" on your display.

If they cannot contact you they will leave a message to say they are sending you an appointment letter.

The letter will tell you:

  • exactly where to go
  • who you will be seeing on the day
  • what will happen during the appointment
  • what to do if you need to rearrange the appointment

Tell the clinic reception staff when they call if you need an interpreter for your appointment.

Things to look out for while you wait for your appointment

If you:

  • notice your breast is more painful, swollen, warm or red (which may be harder to see on black or brown skin)
  • develop a high temperature
  • feel confused
  • want to be sick (vomit)

You should ask for an urgent (same day) GP appointment.

If you cannot see your GP, go to a local urgent treatment centre (UTC).

What happens during the appointment

You can take a friend or relative with you if you like.

You will be seen in a private room and will need to be bare-chested.

A doctor or nurse will examine your chest using their hands. The doctor or nurse could be male or female.

You should expect to spend no more than 3 hours at the clinic.

Tests you might have

You might have a breast X-ray (which is called a mammogram for men too). If you need ultrasound or other tests they can all be done at the clinic.

Sometimes you might need to have a biopsy. This is where a small needle is inserted into the breast tissue to collect some cells from the lump. The cells are checked under the microscope.

You will usually have all the tests you need on the same day. Not everyone needs all the tests.

You can read more about breast cancer diagnosis in men on the Breast Cancer Now website.

Getting your results

The breast clinic will tell you how and when you will get your results. Any results will be given in person or over the phone.

You might get some results later the same day, others can take a week or more.

The whole process from referral to knowing whether or not you have cancer should take no more than 28 days.

Get advice and support

It's normal to feel anxious before an appointment but most men who are referred to a clinic do not have cancer.

You do not need to have been diagnosed with breast cancer to get support from specialist charities.

The Macmillan website has information about breast cancer in men. You can also get support from their online community.

You can email or call Breast Cancer Now's specialist nurses for advice or read more information on their website about where to get support.
